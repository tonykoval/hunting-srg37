An Interactive Hunt Log Hunting SRG(37)

There are exactly $6802$ strongly regular graphs with parameters $(37, 18, 8, 9)$ that anyone has ever written down. Is there a $6803$rd? Nobody knows. This book is the record of twelve attempts to find one.

The one-sentence problem

A strongly regular graph $\mathrm{SRG}(n, k, \lambda, \mu)$ is a $k$-regular graph on $n$ vertices in which adjacent vertices share exactly $\lambda$ common neighbours and non-adjacent vertices share exactly $\mu$. The tuple $(37, 18, 8, 9)$ is the smallest conference-graph parameter set whose graphs have never been fully classified.

The motivating question

The Paley graph $P(37)$ proves an $\mathrm{SRG}(37, 18, 8, 9)$ exists. Spence (2001), Crnković–Maksimović (2020) and Maksimović–Rukavina (2022) together exhibit $6802$ of them up to isomorphism. But McKay and Spence (2001) explicitly left over a million spectrum-compatible neighbour graphs unsearched. So the question is not does one exist — it is is the list of $6802$ complete? Equivalently: can you find a $6803$rd?

What this book is

Not a textbook and not a triumph. It is a hunt log: an honest, reproducible account of twelve distinct attacks on the $(37, 18, 8, 9)$ wall, every one of which returned zero new graphs. The attacks span five families — SAT and constraint solving, combinatorial switching, local and continuous optimisation, large-language-model construction, and custom backtracking. Each chapter explains the idea, shows the encoding or algorithm, reports the compute budget and the result, and extracts the lesson that survives the failure.

A negative result, carefully bounded, is still a result. By the end you will have a precise map of where the wall is, why every classical method bounces off it, and what a serious assault on $\#6803$ would actually require.

How to read

Online, chapters carry interactive widgets: a live feasibility calculator pre-loaded with $(37, 18, 8, 9)$, a Paley$(37)$ viewer, an SRG verifier you can paste a candidate into, and a filterable scoreboard of all twelve attacks. For paper, the print edition stitches every chapter into one document; Ctrl+P → Save as PDF yields a clean monograph.

Map of the book

Part 0 — Math for Dummies. An optional primer on eigenvalues, the spectrum of a matrix, and the trace — every linear-algebra idea Chapter 2 leans on. Skip it if you already know this material.

Part I — The Target. What $(37, 18, 8, 9)$ is, why it is a conference graph, what its spectrum forces, and exactly how the $6802$-graph catalog was assembled and verified.

Part II — The Hunt. The twelve attacks, grouped by family: SAT/CEGAR/cube-and-conquer (Ch 4), Godsil–McKay switching (Ch 5), simulated annealing / basin-hopping / Euclidean embedding (Ch 6), five LLM frameworks (Ch 7), and a bitset backtracker (Ch 8).

Part III — Findings & Frontier. What the hunt actually established (Ch 9), what would be needed to break the wall — distributed cube-and-conquer, a Rust extension backtracker, or a completeness proof (Ch 10), and the Docker images, scripts and data that make every attack reproducible (Ch 11).
