Chapter 0.1 · Part 0 — Math for Dummies Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

This short part exists so the rest of the book is self-contained. If you already know what eigenvalues, the spectrum, and the trace are, skip straight to Chapter 1. Otherwise, three gentle chapters and you will have everything Chapter 2 needs.

0.1.1   A matrix is a machine that moves vectors

A vector is just an arrow from the origin — a list of numbers, like $\binom{3}{1}$ for "3 right, 1 up". A matrix is a square table of numbers, and its one job is to take a vector in and hand a (usually different) vector out. You feed $v$ in, you get $Av$ out. That output is computed by the rule "row by column": $$ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ax + by \\ cx + dy \end{pmatrix}. $$

For a graph, the matrix we care about is the adjacency matrix $A$: a grid of $0$s and $1$s with a $1$ in row $i$, column $j$ exactly when vertices $i$ and $j$ are joined. Everything this book does with graphs, it does by studying what their adjacency matrix does to vectors.

Geometrically, multiplying by a matrix usually does two things at once: it rotates the arrow to point somewhere new, and it stretches or shrinks its length. For most input arrows, both happen.

0.1.2   Eigenvectors: the arrows that refuse to turn

A few special arrows are different. For them the matrix does not rotate the arrow at all — it only stretches it (or flips it around). The arrow comes out pointing along the very same line it went in on. Such an arrow is called an eigenvector, and the amount it gets stretched by is its eigenvalue.

Definition — eigenvalue & eigenvector

A nonzero vector $v$ is an eigenvector of the matrix $A$ if $$ A\,v \;=\; \lambda\, v $$ for some number $\lambda$, called the corresponding eigenvalue. In words: "$A$ applied to $v$ is just $v$ scaled by $\lambda$." A positive $\lambda$ stretches, a $\lambda$ between $0$ and $1$ shrinks, a negative $\lambda$ flips, and $\lambda = 0$ collapses $v$ onto the origin.

0.1.4   Finding them by hand

$Av = \lambda v$ rearranges to $(A - \lambda I)v = 0$, where $I$ is the identity matrix (1s on the diagonal, 0s elsewhere — the "do nothing" matrix). A nonzero $v$ can satisfy this only when $A - \lambda I$ is singular, i.e. its determinant is zero. That gives the characteristic equation. For a $2\times 2$ matrix $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} a & b \\ b & c \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ it is just a quadratic: $$ \det\!\begin{pmatrix} a-\lambda & b \\ b & c-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = \lambda^2 - (a+c)\lambda + (ac - b^2) = 0. $$

Worked: $A = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$

The equation is $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$, i.e. $(\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 1) = 0$, so $\lambda = 3$ or $\lambda = 1$. The eigenvector for $\lambda = 3$ is $\binom{1}{1}$ (check: $A\binom{1}{1} = \binom{3}{3} = 3\binom{1}{1}$); for $\lambda = 1$ it is $\binom{1}{-1}$. Two perpendicular directions, each merely scaled. Set $a=c=2,\ b=1$ in the explorer above to watch exactly this.

0.1.5   Why this is the whole game for SRGs

Here is the punchline that powers the entire book. A graph is strongly regular if and only if its adjacency matrix has only three different eigenvalues. Three numbers, no matter how many vertices. For our target $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ those three are $18$ and the two roots of a quadratic (Chapter 2). An ordinary $37$-vertex graph would have up to $37$ scattered eigenvalues; the strong regularity crushes them down to three. That collapse is the structure we exploit — and the thing every attack in Part II is ultimately fighting.
