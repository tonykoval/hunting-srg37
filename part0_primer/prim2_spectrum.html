Chapter 0.2 · Part 0 — Math for Dummies The Spectrum of a Matrix

One eigenvalue is a fact about one direction. The spectrum is the whole collection of them at once — and for the matrices in this book it is a short, rigid list that says almost everything worth knowing.

0.2.1   All the eigenvalues, with repeats

An $n \times n$ matrix has $n$ eigenvalues (counted with repetition). The spectrum is that multiset — the list of eigenvalues together with how many times each occurs. A repeated eigenvalue's count is its multiplicity: it tells you the number of independent directions that the matrix merely scales by that value.

Definition — spectrum & multiplicity

The spectrum of $A$ is the multiset of its eigenvalues. If a value $\lambda$ appears $m$ times we write $\lambda^{(m)}$ and call $m$ its multiplicity. We list spectra largest-first, e.g. $\{\,3^{(1)},\ 1^{(5)},\ (-2)^{(4)}\,\}$ for a $10$-vertex graph.

0.2.2   Symmetric matrices are the friendly ones

Adjacency matrices are symmetric — the entry in row $i$, column $j$ equals the one in row $j$, column $i$, because "$i$ joined to $j$" is the same statement as "$j$ joined to $i$". Symmetric matrices have two gifts (the spectral theorem):

  • all their eigenvalues are real numbers — never imaginary;
  • their eigenvectors can be chosen mutually perpendicular, forming a complete set of axes for the whole space.

So a graph's spectrum is always a clean list of real numbers, and the eigenvectors give a natural coordinate system in which the matrix is "diagonal" — pure stretching, no mixing. That is exactly why eigenvalues are such a powerful lens.

The pentagon's irrational spectrum

$C_5$ has spectrum $\{\,2^{(1)},\ \bigl(\tfrac{-1+\sqrt5}{2}\bigr)^{(2)}, \ \bigl(\tfrac{-1-\sqrt5}{2}\bigr)^{(2)}\,\}$ — three distinct values, two of them irrational, with equal multiplicities $2 = 2$. That is the pentagon being the smallest conference graph, the same family as our target. Swap $5$ for $37$ and the multiplicities become $18 = 18$ — the structure of Chapter 2 in miniature.

0.2.4   The spectrum of $(37,18,8,9)$

For our target the spectrum is $$ \Bigl\{\,18^{(1)},\ \Bigl(\tfrac{-1+\sqrt{37}}{2}\Bigr)^{(18)}, \ \Bigl(\tfrac{-1-\sqrt{37}}{2}\Bigr)^{(18)}\,\Bigr\}. $$ Three distinct values; the degree $18$ once, and the two irrational eigenvalues eighteen times each. $1 + 18 + 18 = 37$ accounts for every vertex. The astonishing part — proven in Chapter 2 — is that you can read this entire list off the four parameters $(37,18,8,9)$ without ever building a graph. The spectrum is determined before the graph exists.

There is one more number hiding in every spectrum, and it is the easiest of all to compute: the trace.
