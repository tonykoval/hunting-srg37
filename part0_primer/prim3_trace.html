Chapter 0.3 · Part 0 — Math for Dummies The Trace

The trace is the simplest number you can extract from a matrix — just add up the diagonal. Its secret is that it equals the sum of the eigenvalues, which turns "add a diagonal" into a powerful constraint on a spectrum you cannot see directly.

0.3.1   Add the diagonal. That's it.

Definition — trace

The trace of a square matrix $A$, written $\operatorname{tr}(A)$, is the sum of its diagonal entries $A_{11} + A_{22} + \cdots + A_{nn}$. No eigenvalues, no work — just read down the diagonal and add.

0.3.2   The secret: trace = sum of eigenvalues

Here is the fact that makes the trace useful far beyond its definition: $$ \operatorname{tr}(A) \;=\; \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n, $$ the sum of all the eigenvalues (with multiplicity). The diagonal you can see and the spectrum you often cannot are forced to add up to the same total. More generally, $\operatorname{tr}(A^k)$ equals $\sum_i \lambda_i^{\,k}$ — the $k$-th powers of the eigenvalues — which gives a whole ladder of constraints.

0.3.3   For a graph, the trace is zero

A simple graph has no self-loops, so the diagonal of its adjacency matrix is all zeros. Therefore $$ \operatorname{tr}(A) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sum_i \lambda_i = 0. $$ The eigenvalues of any graph sum to zero. A second freebie: $\operatorname{tr}(A^2)$ counts closed walks of length two, which is just twice the number of edges (equivalently the sum of the degrees), and it also equals $\sum_i \lambda_i^2$.

0.3.4   Check it on a real graph

For each graph the widget shows the diagonal sum, the sum of the eigenvalues, and the same comparison for $A^2$. The two ways of computing each total always agree — that is the theorem, live.

0.3.5   How Chapter 2 uses it

The trace is the lever that pins down the multiplicities of $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$. Its spectrum is $18$ once, $r$ with multiplicity $f$, and $s$ with multiplicity $g$. Trace zero says $$ 18 + f\,r + g\,s = 0. $$ Combined with $f + g = 36$ (the two non-trivial eigenspaces fill the remaining $36$ dimensions) and $r + s = \lambda - \mu = -1$, this forces $$ f = g = 18. $$ Plugging back: $18 + 18r + 18s = 18 + 18(r+s) = 18 + 18(-1) = 0$ ✓. That balanced split $18 = 18$, dictated entirely by the trace, is the defining feature of the conference case — and the reason the eigenvalues are allowed to be irrational. You met the same balance as $2 = 2$ on the pentagon in Chapter 0.2.

You now have the toolkit

Eigenvalues (how a matrix scales special directions), the spectrum (all of them, with multiplicities), and the trace (their sum, free from the diagonal). That is every linear-algebra idea Chapter 2 relies on. Onward to the target.
