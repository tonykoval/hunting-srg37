Chapter 1 · Part I The Target

Four numbers — $(37, 18, 8, 9)$ — pin down a graph so rigid that once you fix any vertex, the entire local structure is determined. The remarkable thing is not that such graphs exist. It is that we cannot say how many.

1.1   The definition, in one line

Fix four integers $(n, k, \lambda, \mu)$. A graph $\Gamma$ on $n$ vertices is strongly regular with these parameters when:

  1. $\Gamma$ is $k$-regular — every vertex has exactly $k$ neighbours;
  2. every two adjacent vertices share exactly $\lambda$ common neighbours;
  3. every two non-adjacent vertices share exactly $\mu$ common neighbours.
Definition 1.1 — Strongly regular graph

A $k$-regular graph $\Gamma$ on $n$ vertices is an $\mathrm{SRG}(n, k, \lambda, \mu)$ if its adjacency matrix $A$ satisfies $$ A^2 \;=\; k\,I + \lambda\,A + \mu\,(J - I - A), $$ with $I$ the identity and $J$ the all-ones matrix. The $(u,v)$ entry of $A^2$ counts walks of length two; the three regularity conditions are exactly the three cases $u=v$, $u\sim v$, $u\not\sim v$.

Our target is the single tuple $(n, k, \lambda, \mu) = (37, 18, 8, 9)$: $37$ vertices, every vertex of degree $18$, every edge in $8$ triangles, every non-edge closed by $9$ common neighbours.

1.2   A graph that exists

Existence is not in doubt. The Paley graph $P(37)$ is an $\mathrm{SRG}(37, 18, 8, 9)$: take the vertices to be the integers modulo $37$, and join $u \sim v$ exactly when $u - v$ is a nonzero quadratic residue (a perfect square) mod $37$. Because $37 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, the residue $-1$ is itself a square, so the relation is symmetric and the graph is undirected.

So the existence question — is there an $\mathrm{SRG}(37, 18, 8, 9)$? — was settled the moment Paley wrote down his construction in 1933. Brouwer's parameter table marks the row with a +: a graph is known. What the + does not tell you is the number of such graphs up to isomorphism, and that count is the heart of this book.

1.3   Why $(37, 18, 8, 9)$, and why $\#6803$

$(37, 18, 8, 9)$ is the smallest member of the conference-graph family — the parameters $\bigl(n, \tfrac{n-1}{2}, \tfrac{n-5}{4}, \tfrac{n-1}{4}\bigr)$ with $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ — for which the graphs have never been completely enumerated. The smaller conference cases are settled: there is a unique $\mathrm{SRG}(5,2,0,1)$ (the pentagon), a unique $(9,4,1,2)$ (the $3\times 3$ rook graph $=P(9)$), a unique $(13,6,2,3)$ ($=P(13)$), and exactly $15$ graphs on $(25,12,5,6)$ and $10$ on $(29,14,6,7)$. At $n = 37$ the count explodes and the classification breaks down.

The best published lower bound on that count is $\boldsymbol{6802}$. A natural reading of the literature — Spence's $6760$ graphs, plus a handful added by later authors — might suggest the list is "essentially complete". It is not. As we detail in Chapter 3, McKay and Spence (2001) were explicit that over a million spectrum-compatible candidate neighbourhoods were never searched. The honest status is:

Status of $(37,18,8,9)$

Existence: known (Paley$(37)$, since 1933).
Count: at least $6802$ up to isomorphism; completeness is open.
The hunt: exhibit a $6803$rd graph — a valid $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ isomorphic to none of the $6802$ — or accumulate evidence that none exists.

A $6803$rd graph would be a small, publishable result: a new point in a classification that has stood incomplete since 2001. That is the prize this book hunts.

1.4   The verifier we will use everywhere

Every construction attempt in Part II ends the same way: build a $37 \times 37$ adjacency matrix, then check it. Here is the complete test, unchanged for the rest of the book. 

import numpy as np

def is_srg(A, n=37, k=18, lam=8, mu=9):
    """True iff A (n×n, 0/1, symmetric, zero diagonal) is SRG(n,k,λ,μ)."""
    if A.shape != (n, n):          return False
    if not np.array_equal(A, A.T): return False
    if np.any(np.diag(A) != 0):    return False
    if np.any(A.sum(axis=1) != k): return False
    J = np.ones((n, n), dtype=int)
    I = np.eye(n,        dtype=int)
    rhs = k*I + lam*A + mu*(J - I - A)        # A² = kI + λA + μ(J−I−A)
    return np.array_equal(A @ A, rhs)

Passing is_srg is necessary but not sufficient for a discovery: a candidate must also be non-isomorphic to all $6802$ known graphs. Chapter 3 supplies the second test; together they define what "$\#6803$" means.

1.5   What follows

Chapter 2 reads the spectrum off the parameters and shows why $(37,18,8,9)$ is a conference graph with irrational eigenvalues and balanced multiplicities $18 = 18$ — and why every classical feasibility filter passes, so non-existence is not on the table. Chapter 3 assembles the $6802$-graph catalog from its three sources, verifies it against the published classifications, and quantifies exactly how much of the search space remains untouched. Then Part II opens the hunt.
