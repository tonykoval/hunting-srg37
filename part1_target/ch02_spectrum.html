Chapter 2 · Part I Spectrum & Feasibility

Before any computer runs, the parameters $(37, 18, 8, 9)$ already tell us almost everything: the eigenvalues, their multiplicities, and the fact that no classical obstruction can rule the graph out. This is why the hunt is for a count, never an existence proof.

2.1   Eigenvalues from the parameters

The defining identity $A^2 = kI + \lambda A + \mu(J - I - A)$ means $A$ commutes with $J$, so they share eigenvectors. The all-ones vector $\mathbf{1}$ gives the eigenvalue $k$. On the space orthogonal to $\mathbf{1}$ (where $J = 0$) the identity collapses to $A^2 = (\lambda - \mu)A + (k - \mu)I$, so the remaining eigenvalues solve $$ x^2 - (\lambda - \mu)\,x - (k - \mu) = 0. $$ Writing the two roots $r > s$, $$ r, s \;=\; \frac{(\lambda-\mu) \pm \sqrt{(\lambda-\mu)^2 + 4(k-\mu)}}{2}. $$

Worked: $(37,18,8,9)$

Here $\lambda - \mu = -1$ and $k - \mu = 9$, so the quadratic is $x^2 + x - 9 = 0$ with discriminant $1 + 36 = 37$. Thus $$ r = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{37}}{2} \approx 2.5414, \qquad s = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{37}}{2} \approx -3.5414. $$ The non-principal eigenvalues are irrational — a hallmark of the conference case below.

2.2   The conference case

$(37,18,8,9)$ belongs to the conference family: parameters of the shape $$ \Bigl(n,\; \tfrac{n-1}{2},\; \tfrac{n-5}{4},\; \tfrac{n-1}{4}\Bigr), \qquad n \equiv 1 \pmod 4, $$ obtained here with $n = 37$. The signature feature is that $\lambda - \mu = -1$ and $2k + (n-1)(\lambda-\mu) = 0$. The general multiplicity formula $$ f, g \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\left[(n-1) \mp \frac{2k + (n-1)(\lambda - \mu)}{\sqrt{(\lambda-\mu)^2 + 4(k-\mu)}}\right] $$ then has a vanishing correction term, forcing $$ f = g = \frac{n-1}{2} = 18. $$

Equal multiplicities with irrational eigenvalues is the half case. It is consistent precisely because $f = g$ keeps the trace integral: $\operatorname{tr} A = k + f r + g s = 18 + 18(r + s) = 18 + 18(-1) = 0$, as it must be for a zero-diagonal matrix. So the spectrum is $$ \mathrm{Spec}(A) = \Bigl\{\,18^{1},\; \bigl(\tfrac{-1+\sqrt{37}}{2}\bigr)^{18},\; \bigl(\tfrac{-1-\sqrt{37}}{2}\bigr)^{18}\,\Bigr\}. $$

2.3   Every feasibility filter passes

A parameter tuple can be killed before any search by a battery of classical conditions. For $(37,18,8,9)$ each one is satisfied — unsurprising, since Paley$(37)$ realises the parameters, but worth seeing explicitly because it tells the searcher that non-existence is off the table.

ConditionRequirement$(37,18,8,9)$
Handshake$k(k-\lambda-1) = (n-k-1)\mu$$18\cdot 9 = 18\cdot 9$ ✓
Integral multiplicities$f, g \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$$f = g = 18$ ✓
Trace$k + fr + gs = 0$$18 + 18(-1) = 0$ ✓
Krein (both)$(r{+}1)(k{+}r{+}2rs) \le (k{+}r)(s{+}1)^2$, & dualholds ✓
Absolute bound$n \le \tfrac{1}{2}f(f+3)$$37 \le 189$ ✓
Hoffman / Delsarte clique$\omega \le 1 - k/s$$\omega \le 6.08$, no clash ✓

There is a clean structural reason all of these hold at once: a conference graph is self-complementary in parameters — the complement of an $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ is again an $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ — and conference parameters with integral $f = g$ are known to be feasible whenever $n$ is a sum of two squares, which $37 = 36 + 1$ is. The conditions that kill other open tuples (a non-integral multiplicity, a violated Krein bound, an absolute -bound overflow) simply have no purchase here.

Consequence for the hunt

Because no obstruction applies, you cannot hope to "prove $\#6803$ does not exist" by a feasibility argument — the parameters are fully feasible and a graph demonstrably exists. The only questions on the table are constructive (build a new one) or enumerative (prove the list of $6802$ is exhaustive). Part II pursues the first; Chapter 10 discusses the second.

2.5   Where this leaves us

The spectrum is fixed, the multiplicities are balanced, and the tuple is fully feasible. The graph is real. What is unknown is purely a matter of how many — and that question lives not in the spectrum but in the combinatorics of fitting $18$-regular neighbourhoods together. Chapter 3 turns to the $6802$ graphs we do have, how they were found, and how much of the space they leave unexplored.
