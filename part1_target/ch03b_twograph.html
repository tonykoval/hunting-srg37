Chapter 3½ · Part I The Two-Graph Reduction — Where #6803 Lives

Chapter 3 said the catalog is a lower bound and any new graph is rigid. This chapter says where the rigid graph must be. The hunt is not really for a 37-vertex graph at all — it is for one extra regular two-graph on 38 points, and a clean lemma plus a completed classification pin it to a single, well-defined corner of the search space.

3½.1   One object, four disguises

A conference graph sits at the bottom of a chain of equivalent objects. Finding any one of them solves the problem.

The operational target is the conference matrix. A symmetric $C$ of order 38 with $C^2 = 37I$ is a regular two-graph on 38 points, and every one of its 38 vertex descendants is an SRG(37,18,8,9). So the entire problem reduces to:

The target, in one line

Find a symmetric $\pm1$ matrix $C$ of order 38, zero diagonal, with $C^2 = 37I$, whose descendant is not among the 6802 catalog graphs — that descendant is $\#6803$.

The conference relation, interactively

The relation $C^2 = (n{-}1)I$ is rigid: every off-diagonal entry of $C^2$ must be exactly $0$. Below is a genuine symmetric conference matrix of order 6 (so $C^2 = 5I$). Flip any off-diagonal $\pm1$ and watch the off-diagonal of $C^2$ light up — a single change wrecks the relation everywhere. This is exactly the "defect" the order-38 search (and the GPU finder of Chapter 10) must drive to zero.

3½.2   Prescribed symmetry is tiny or empty: the $p\!\cdot\!f \le 37$ lemma

Before the brute search, ask whether a new two-graph could carry an automorphism we can exploit. For a single prime-order automorphism the answer is sharply bounded.

Lemma  ($p\cdot f \le 37$)

Let a regular two-graph on 38 points admit an automorphism $\sigma$ of prime order $p$ fixing $f$ points (so $38 - f \equiv 0 \bmod p$). Then $\;p\cdot f \le 37$.

Proof sketch. $\sigma$ commutes with the Seidel matrix $S$ ($S^2 = 37I$), so $S$ preserves the $\sigma$-invariant subspace spanned by the $f$ fixed-point indicators and the normalised orbit-sum vectors. The compression $M_0$ of $S$ to that subspace still satisfies $M_0^2 = 37I$. Reading the diagonal of this identity on the fixed block and bounding the off-block terms (sums of squares of orbit row-sums, $\ge 0$) gives $p\cdot f + (\text{nonneg}) = 37$, hence $p\cdot f \le 37$. $\;\square$

Combine the lemma with the parity $38 - f \equiv 0 \pmod p$ and the complete classification of symmetric SRG(37,18,8,9) (the 40 of Chapter 3), and the prescribed-cyclic landscape collapses entirely:

  • The only fixed-point-free cyclic cases are $p=2,f=0$ (proven impossible, by a determinant/row-sum parity argument) and $p=19,f=0$ (done — five classes, all in the catalog).
  • Every cyclic case with $f>0$ has a symmetric descendant at a fixed vertex, and is therefore already inside the complete 40-graph classification.

So no prescribed-cyclic route can reach an uncovered graph. (Empirically confirmed: a 60-second annealing probe of the smallest open cyclic case, $p=7,f=3$ with 103 free bits, floors at ${\sim}1004$ violations and finds nothing.)

3½.3   The three-layer map

Putting the rigidity theorem (Chapter 3) and the lemma together, the regular two-graphs on 38 split into exactly three layers — and only one is open.

The exact open question

Does a 195th regular two-graph on 38 points, with trivial automorphism group, exist? The 194 known two-graphs yield the 6802 catalog graphs; a 195th would yield new descendants — and the first of those not already in the catalog is $\#6803$. The McKay–Spence enumeration of regular two-graphs on 38 is the first open case (everything up to 36 is classified), so this is a genuine, named, unsolved problem — not a fishing expedition.

This is the justification for everything in Part II and III: the search must be isomorph-free enumeration over fully asymmetric two-graphs on 38. Chapter 4's SMS+CCL cube-and-conquer is exactly that tool; the rest of the book is the record of which shortcuts to it do and do not work.
