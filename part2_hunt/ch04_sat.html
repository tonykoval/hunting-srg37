Chapter 4 · Part II SAT & Constraint Attacks

The most direct attack is to write "$\Gamma$ is an $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$" as a Boolean formula and hand it to a solver. Four variants were tried, from CEGAR over symmetric seeds to cube-and-conquer with a canonicity propagator. All four hit the same structural wall — but one of them showed exactly where the wall is thin.

4.1   Encoding an SRG as a formula

Represent the graph by one Boolean variable per potential edge: $x_{ij}$ for $0 \le i < j \le 36$, so $\binom{37}{2} = 666$ edge variables. The defining conditions become:

  • Degree. For each vertex $i$, $\sum_{j \ne i} x_{ij} = 18$ — a cardinality constraint.
  • Common neighbours. For each pair $(i,j)$, $\sum_{t} x_{it}\,x_{jt} = 8$ if $x_{ij}=1$, else $9$. The product $x_{it}\,x_{jt}$ is an AND that needs an auxiliary variable $y_{ijt}$ with clauses $y \to x_{it}$, $y \to x_{jt}$, $x_{it}\wedge x_{jt}\to y$.

The trouble is in the counting. Each of the $666$ pairs needs a cardinality constraint over up to $35$ product terms, and each product needs its own variable and three clauses. Encoding the cardinalities (Sinz sequential counters, or totalizers) multiplies everything again. The result is enormous:

Encoding sizes actually observed

A Seidel-style direct encoding of $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$: $\approx 110{,}000$ variables, $\approx 230{,}000$ clauses. A neighbour-extension encoding (fix one $(18,8)$ neighbourhood, solve for the rest): $\approx 910{,}000$ variables, $\approx 1{,}870{,}000$ clauses. These are not solver-specific; the same blow-up appears whether the back end is Google OR-Tools CP-SAT or the SAT-competition winner kissat.

4.2   Attack 1 — de Caen survey over symmetric seeds

Rather than solve the full symmetric problem, restrict to graphs sharing an automorphism with a known one. For each of the $40$ nontrivial-Aut catalog graphs, split at its best vertex (smallest automorphism orbit = largest stabiliser), encode de Caen's linear $B$-block constraints with an automorphism-aware lex-leader symmetry break, and run a CEGAR loop (refine on violated common-neighbour counts) through CryptoMiniSat.

BudgetSeedsResult
$180$ s/graph$40$all time out (Wall A)

Every instance produced the same outcome — no model, no refutation — across automorphism products $\{1, 81, 1296\}$, all at the $\sim\!110\text{K}$-variable scale. The recommendation for a serious run is to push the per-graph budget to $3600$ s or beyond, roughly $40$ CPU-hours for the full sweep.

A correctness trap

The tempting "complement-swap" symmetry break (negate $B$ for a self-complementary SRG) is unsound unless the chosen self-complementing permutation $\pi$ satisfies $\pi^2 = \mathrm{id}$. For Paley$(37)$, $\pi$ is multiplication by a non-residue and has order $> 2$, so the naive break introduces false fixed-point contradictions. Do not enable it without checking $\pi^2 = \mathrm{id}$.

4.3   Attack 2 — Smart Cubing

The Kirchweger–Szeider Smart Cubing recipe (arXiv:2501.17201) reports $4$–$6\times$ speedups on graph-search SAT instances. The pipeline: a CaDiCaL prerun with DRAT proof tracing to harvest short ($\le 5$-literal) learned clauses; enrich the formula with them; an activity-based cuber that emits ${\sim}1024$ cubes; and a parallel conquer with kissat workers. 

# Three stages, as run in the hunt
smart-cube-prerun  --cnf seidel_srg37.cnf --time 600      # DRAT → enriched.cnf
smart-cube-cuber   --k 10 --drat prerun.drat              # → 1024 cubes
smart-cube-conquer --workers 6 --time-limit 60            # kissat per cube
CubesPer-cube budgetResult
${\sim}1024$$60$ sall UNKNOWN ($17$ worker-hours, $0$ SAT)

The paper's gains depend on dynamic SAT-modulo-symmetry propagation during search. Static automorphism-aware symmetry breaking plus post-hoc DRAT-learned clauses does not reproduce it: the cubes are no easier than the original formula. This is the key negative lesson of the chapter — the symmetry breaking has to be dynamic.

4.4   Attack 3 — SMS+CCL cube-and-conquer (the bright spot)

The dynamic version. SAT modulo symmetry with a complete canonicity check (CCL): a C-nauty propagator wired through the IPASIR-UP interface adds, mid-search, the clause "this partial assignment is not lexicographically minimal in its isomorphism class". We attack the equivalent regular two-graph on $38$ vertices, split into $2^{5} = 32$ cubes.

CubesResultTime
$32$ (cube size 5)$28$ UNSAT, $4$ still searching$28$ closed in $< 1$ min total

Twenty-eight of thirty-two cubes were refuted almost instantly on a desktop — the canonicity propagator prunes isomorphic branches brilliantly. Only four "hot-spot" cubes resisted, and the binary's --timeout flag was not enforced in that build, so they ran unbounded and had to be killed by hand. This is the single most promising classical path: the propagator works; it just needs more cores and a timeout fix. We return to it in Chapter 10.

4.5   Attack 4 — Neighbour extension

A different framing: take one of the $30{,}122$ unaccounted $(18,8)$ neighbour graphs from $\S$3.3, fix it as $N(v)$, and ask SAT to fill in the remaining $18$ vertices consistently. If any extends, it is almost certainly a $\#6803$.

None decided either way. The extension encoding is so large that a minute of solving barely scratches the search tree — neither a model nor a proof of its absence emerges. Chapter 8 returns to extension with a hand-written backtracker instead of a generic solver.

4.6   The lesson of the family

Wall A

For $(37,18,8,9)$, generic SAT/CP encodings are dominated by cardinality constraints on common-neighbour counts, producing $10^5$–$10^6$ clauses. At that size the back-end solver is irrelevant — CP-SAT and kissat fail identically. What changes the picture is not a better solver but a better symmetry story: dynamic canonicity propagation (SMS+CCL) closes $88\%$ of the two-graph cubes instantly, while static breaking plus learned clauses (Smart Cubing) closes none.
