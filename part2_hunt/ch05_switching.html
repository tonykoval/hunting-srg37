Chapter 5 · Part II Combinatorial Switching

Switching takes a graph you have and produces a cospectral one you may not. Because cospectral SRGs are again SRGs with identical parameters, switching is the classic way to walk between graphs in a single parameter set — when it applies. On $(37,18,8,9)$ it almost never does.

5.1   Godsil–McKay switching

Definition 5.1 — GM switching set

Let $\Gamma$ have vertex set $V$. A Godsil–McKay switching set is a subset $C \subseteq V$ such that for every vertex $y \in V \setminus C$, $$ \deg(y, C) \;\in\; \{\,0,\; \tfrac{|C|}{2},\; |C|\,\}, $$ i.e. $y$ is joined to none, exactly half, or all of $C$. Switching replaces the edges between $C$ and each "half" vertex $y$ by their complement. The result is cospectral with $\Gamma$; if $\Gamma$ is an SRG, so is the switched graph, with the same parameters.

If a switching set exists and the switched graph is non-isomorphic to every catalog graph, you have a discovery — for free, no search. Seidel's two-graph switching is the $|C| = 2$ special case, and it is in this language that the regular two-graphs on $38$ vertices (Chapter 4) live. The hope: some catalog graph has a switching set leading out of the catalog.

5.2   Attack 5 — single-cell switching

For Paley$(37)$ and all $40$ nontrivial-automorphism catalog graphs, we searched for switching sets $C$ of even sizes $4, 6, 8, 10, 12$, applied the switch, canonicalised, and compared to the catalog.

Cell size $|C|$SearchValid sets foundNew graphs
$4$exhaustive, $40$ graphs, $138$ s$0$$0$
$6$sampled, ${\sim}20$ min$0$$0$

The result is stark: no valid switching set of any tested size exists, on any catalog graph sampled. The condition $\deg(y, C) \in \{0, |C|/2, |C|\}$ has to hold simultaneously for all $37 - |C|$ outside vertices, and on a graph this uniformly knit, some vertex always lands at an intermediate degree like $1, 3$ or $5$. The regularity that defines the graph is exactly what forbids the switch.

5.3   Attack 6 — multi-cell switching

GM switching also works with a partition into several cells, each independently satisfying the half-condition. We tried two-cell partitions of sizes $(4,4)$, $(4,6)$ and $(6,6)$ across $20$ catalog SRGs, with $50{,}000$ random samples per configuration.

Multi-cell is more restrictive, not less: every cell must satisfy the half-condition, so requiring two at once compounds the rarity. Seidel pair switching (the $|C| = 2$ case) likewise yielded $0$ new graphs.

Why switching fails here

A useful intuition: switching sets are abundant in sparse or highly imprimitive graphs, where many vertices see a cell "all-or-nothing". An $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ is the opposite — primitive, half-density ($k = (n-1)/2$), and rigid. The defining arithmetic ($\lambda = 8$, $\mu = 9$) pins inter-cell degrees to values that are almost never exactly $0$, $|C|/2$, or $|C|$ for all outside vertices at once.

5.4   Verdict

Empirical finding

$\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ catalog graphs are rigid under small-cell Godsil–McKay switching: no valid single cell of size $\le 12$ and no valid two-cell partition of sizes up to $(6,6)$ was found across the symmetric catalog graphs and millions of samples. Switching, the cheapest route to a sibling graph, is closed at these scales. Larger cells ($|C| \ge 14$) are combinatorially explosive and were not exhausted — a possible but expensive frontier.

Switching assumed we could move between nearby graphs. The next chapter drops that assumption and searches the space of all $0/1$ matrices directly, by optimisation.
