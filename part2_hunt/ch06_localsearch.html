Chapter 6 · Part II Local & Continuous Search

Treat "be an SRG" as a cost to minimise and let an optimiser hunt for a zero. Three optimisers were tried — greedy basin-hopping, simulated annealing, and gradient descent on a geometric embedding. Each found a floor it could not cross, and the floor sits far above zero.

6.1   The objective

Work in the space of $18$-regular graphs on $37$ vertices and count defects. For every pair $(i,j)$ let $c_{ij}$ be its number of common neighbours; the target is $8$ on edges and $9$ on non-edges. 6.3   Attack 7 — catalog-anchored basin-hopping

Instead of cold starts, anchor on the catalog. Take a known graph, perturb it by a controlled number of swaps, then run Lin–Kernighan-style chained local search (depth $2$) to fall into a nearby basin — hoping the basin is a different SRG.

A separate probe is even more telling: perturb a catalog graph by a few $2$-swaps and hill-climb back. With $5$–$8$ swaps the search recovers the original graph $6$ times in $20$; at $20$ swaps it escapes the basin entirely and never returns to any SRG. The catalog graphs sit in narrow, steep basins separated by a high violation ridge — easy to fall back into, essentially impossible to hop between.

6.4   Attack 8 — simulated annealing

Annealing should escape the basins greedy search cannot. We ran Metropolis SA with deep perturbations ($30$–$60$ swaps) and a geometric schedule $T: 8.0 \to 0.005$.

Trials × stepsNew graphsBest violation
$120 \times 50{,}000$$0$$\ge 340$ (all $120$ stuck)

The initial temperature $T = 8$ turned out to be too cold relative to the scale of $\Delta\mathrm{viol}$ for a single move, so SA behaved like greedy descent and parked at the same floor. An earlier six-hour run on a partial-difference-set fitness plateaued analogously at $f \approx 1602$. Hotter starts and reheating are untried levers, but the basin geometry of $\S$6.3 suggests they would need to be dramatic.

6.5   Attack 9 — continuous embedding

A change of space. The $s$-eigenspace of an $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ gives a unit-vector representation: $37$ points on a sphere in $\mathbb{R}^{18}$ whose pairwise inner products take just two values. Optimise those vectors directly with Adam, driving a loss $L_{\text{dist}}$ that rewards the two-distance structure, then threshold to a $0/1$ graph.

The continuous loss falls nicely; the binarised graph is garbage. The gap is the point: a near-miss in $\mathbb{R}^{18}$ does not threshold to a near-miss graph, because the SRG constraint is discrete and brittle. Closing it would need a straight-through estimator or a catalog-initialised warm start — neither tried here.

6.6   The transformer footnote

A learned proposer (a small decoder transformer trained on the catalog, in the PatternBoost style) can warm-start local search to a slightly better basin: best violation $297$ versus the cold-start floor of ${\sim}324$, about $8\%$. But six rounds of iterative PatternBoost showed the improvement is stochastic in the band $[284, 299]$ and does not trend toward zero. The floor belongs to the $2$-swap move set, not to the proposer; a better starting point cannot fix a move set that cannot reach the target. (The pure constructive use of language models is Chapter 7.)

Empirical finding

Under $2$-swap moves, the $(37,18,8,9)$ violation landscape has a floor of a few hundred that greedy descent, basin-hopping, cold SA, and transformer-warm-started LS all hit and none cross. Catalog graphs occupy isolated steep basins. Continuous relaxation optimises a different objective whose minima do not binarise to SRGs. Local search, at the budgets tried, does not reach $\#6803$.
