Chapter 7 · Part II LLM-Driven Attacks

Could a large language model simply write down a new $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$? Five frameworks were built to find out, from evolutionary code generation to a four-agent debate. The verdict is clean and a little humbling: at this precision the model either reproduces Paley$(37)$ or produces noise.

7.1   Why one might hope

LLMs have produced genuine combinatorial constructions when the search is guided — AlphaEvolve-style loops that propose, test, and refine code. The SRG verifier of Chapter 1 is a perfect oracle: cheap, total, and unforgiving. The plan was to let a model propose adjacency-generating code or explicit graphs, score them with $\mathrm{viol}$, and evolve toward zero. Five framings were implemented.

The two failure modes

Mode A — rediscover Paley. When the model reasons algebraically, it lands on the one construction it "knows": quadratic residues mod $37$. That is a valid $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ with $\mathrm{viol} = 0$ — and it is $\#1$ in the catalog. Correct, useless for the hunt.
Mode B — produce noise. When the model writes an explicit graph, the best it managed was $\mathrm{viol} = 1148$ — worse than a random $18$-regular graph (${\sim}1000$). The $(37,18,8,9)$ constraint is too tight to satisfy by one-shot generation.

A standalone pure-LLM construction loop made the point quantitatively: fitness stalled at $\approx 0.0002$ across $23$ iterations — no trend toward a solution. The model is a fluent recaller of known mathematics and a poor producer of new $37$-vertex incidence structure.

7.4   LLM mathematics needs a human verifier

A second, sharper lesson concerns the model's reasoning, not its constructions. Across the algebraic frameworks the LLM repeatedly emitted plausible but wrong arguments:

  • A miscomputed Krein parameter ($q^1_{11} \approx -1.41$) presented as a non-existence obstruction for a graph that does not violate Krein at all;
  • The recurring fallacy that "the local neighbourhood of an $\mathrm{SRG}(n,k,\lambda,\mu)$ is itself an $\mathrm{SRG}(k,\lambda,\lambda{-}1,\mu{-}1)$", used to "prove" false statements — caught on one parameter set by a counterexample, missed on another;
  • Fabricated or stale citations to papers that say no such thing.
Operating rule

Every LLM-produced obstruction, citation, and "proof" in this project was treated as a conjecture until checked by hand or by code. The verifier of Chapter 1 catches bad graphs instantly; bad arguments have no such oracle, so manual sanity-checking is mandatory. The model accelerates idea generation; it does not certify anything.

7.5   Verdict

Empirical finding

At reasoning budgets we could afford, current LLMs cannot construct a novel $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$. They converge on Paley$(37)$ (known) or generate invalid graphs with violation exceeding random ($1148 > 1000$). Their value in this hunt is upstream — proposing methods, drafting encodings, surfacing literature — never as a final authority. The most promising unspent idea is the MCTS-with-LLM-policy framework, which couples generation to the cheap verifier instead of asking for a one-shot answer.
