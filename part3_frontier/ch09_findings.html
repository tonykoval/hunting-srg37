Chapter 9 · Part III Empirical Findings

Twelve attacks, five families, zero new graphs. That sentence is the headline result — but the value is in the five precise statements the hunt can now stand behind, and the one boundary it carefully does not cross.

9.1   The headline

Result

Across every attack in Part II — SAT/CEGAR/cube-and-conquer, Godsil–McKay switching, basin-hopping / simulated annealing / Euclidean embedding, five LLM frameworks, and a backtracking extender — no $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ outside the $6802$-graph catalog was found. The catalog is empirically closed under all classical attack families at single-machine, one-day compute budgets.

9.2   Five things we can now assert

1. The catalog is correctly assembled.

The $6802$ count reproduces, graph-for-graph under canonical form, the union of Spence (2001), Crnković–Maksimović (2020) and Maksimović–Rukavina (2022). The automorphism histogram $\{666^1, 18^2, 9^4, 3^{30}, 2^3, 1^{6762}\}$ matches the published $40$ nontrivial-symmetry graphs exactly. Whatever else is uncertain, the baseline is sound.

2. McKay–Spence's "million unsearched" is real, not rhetorical.

In minutes we produced $30{,}122$ spectrum-compatible $(18,8)$ neighbour graphs by single $2$-swaps of catalog neighbourhoods, none of which appears among the catalog's $121{,}326$ realised neighbourhoods. The unexplored region is concrete and easy to enter; it is only deciding its members that is hard.

3. Extension is intractable for generic solvers.

Sixty unaccounted neighbour graphs, each given $30$–$300$ s of CP-SAT or kissat, produced neither a model nor a refutation. The $910\text{K}$-variable extension encoding is simply too large for a generic solver to make measurable progress in minutes.

4. LLMs cannot construct at this precision.

The best explicit graph any framework produced had violation $1148$, worse than a random $18$-regular graph; the only zero-violation outputs were rediscoveries of Paley$(37)$. The $(37,18,8,9)$ axioms are too tight for one-shot generation.

5. The catalog graphs are rigid under small perturbation.

$2$-swap local search, size-$\le 12$ Godsil–McKay switching, Seidel pair switching, and de Caen CEGAR at $180$ s per seed all leave the catalog exactly where it was. The known graphs sit in narrow, steep, isolated basins.

9.3   The boundary we do not cross

What this is not

This is not a proof that the catalog is complete. The million-plus unaccounted $(18,8)$ neighbour graphs genuinely exist and remain undecided; we sampled $30{,}122$ of them and tested none to completion. A single one that extends would overturn the headline. What the hunt establishes is a robust empirical baseline: at one-machine, one-day budgets, the catalog is closed under every classical method we know.

Two honest readings remain open, and the hunt does not adjudicate between them:

  • $\#6803$ exists and is rigid. The completeness confidence is low ($\le 15\%$), and a trivial-automorphism graph would be invisible to every symmetry-exploiting method here. This is the frontier Chapter 10 targets.
  • $6802$ is final. The uniform failure across five independent families is weak evidence for completeness — publishable as a negative result, but not a proof.

FamilyWhy it bounced
SAT / CPCommon-neighbour cardinalities blow the encoding to $10^5$–$10^6$ clauses; solver choice is irrelevant.
SwitchingThe half-condition $\deg(y,C)\in\{0,|C|/2,|C|\}$ almost never holds on a primitive half-density graph.
Local searchThe $2$-swap landscape has a violation floor of a few hundred; basins are isolated.
LLMThe model recalls Paley or emits noise; it cannot satisfy $666$ coupled constraints in one shot.
BacktrackingRight shape, wrong constant: Python at $220$ nodes/s cannot traverse the tree.

Each failure points at its own remedy — more cores and dynamic symmetry for SAT, a compiled extender for backtracking, a verifier-coupled policy for the model. Chapter 10 turns the remedies into a plan.

9.5   What the wall teaches

The failures are not independent accidents; they are two faces of one structural fact. The rigidity theorem (Ch 3, App. C.2) says any $\#6803$ is rigid — $|\mathrm{Aut}|=1$ — and large ($n=37$ at the dense conference ratio $k/n\approx\tfrac12$). Those two properties define exactly the regime in which general methods fail:

The two walls, and their common cause

Wall A (search cost). Generic SAT/CP on a completion of $234$ parity-deep two-graph bits is intractable; the back-end solver is irrelevant. Symmetry would shrink it — but by the theorem there is none to exploit.
Wall B (local-search floor). Annealing, basin-hopping and Lin–Kernighan all plateau $50$–$90\%$ of the way to a solution; the conference graph is switching-rigid, so the manifold has no nearby descent path.
Both walls are the price of asymmetry. A target that is large and rigid is precisely where every general technique bounces — and what is left is isomorph-free enumeration, slow but sound.

This is not special to $(37,18,8,9)$. The same pattern — symmetry-based methods reach only symmetric objects, and raw search drowns at low symmetry — recurs across open strongly regular and conference-matrix problems; an independent 2023 SAT study of the Conway-99 graph reached the identical verdict. The honest, general lesson: when a combinatorial object is both large and asymmetric, the only methods that can decide it are the ones that pay full enumeration cost.
