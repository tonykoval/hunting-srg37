10.2   The plan, ranked

1. Distributed SMS+CCL at cube size $\ge 7$  (highest probability)

The one attack that visibly worked was the canonicity-propagated cube-and-conquer of $\S$4.4: $28$ of $32$ cubes refuted in under a minute. The four survivors need (a) more cubes — split at size $7$ for $128$ pieces, isolating the hot spots — (b) a real cluster, and (c) the --timeout bug fixed so deep cubes are bounded and recycled. Estimated cost: a week of wall-clock on a modest cloud cluster, \$200–\$500. If $\#6803$ is reachable by enumeration, this finds it.

2. Rust extension backtracker with bitset propagation  (most actionable)

We already hold $30{,}122$ concrete unaccounted neighbourhoods ($\S$3.3). A compiled backtracker (popcount(adj[i] & adj[j]) for common neighbours, masked scans for degree saturation) can test thousands of them per CPU-day. Any single extension is a discovery; systematic failure starts to quantify completeness. This needs no cluster and no budget — just the multi-day implementation begun in orbit-gen v1.0.514.

3. Verifier-coupled learned policy  (research-grade)

The LLM frameworks failed by asking for a one-shot answer. Couple generation to the cheap verifier instead: fine-tune a small model on (graph, valid?) pairs and use it as the policy in an MCTS over partial adjacency matrices, expanding only branches the propagator has not killed. This is the unspent llm_mcts_search.py framework; it is the most speculative and the most interesting.

4. A completeness proof  (the other way to win)

Failing to find $\#6803$ is not the same as proving it absent. A genuine classification — closing the million unaccounted neighbourhoods, e.g. via a Lasserre/SDP hierarchy bound or a structured exhaustive two-graph enumeration — would settle the question in the opposite direction. This is an open problem in finite geometry in its own right, and the hardest item on the list.

10.3   Two ways this ends

The two endings

A new graph. Path 1 or 2 yields a valid $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ whose canonical form is absent from the catalog. The count becomes (at least) $6803$ and the classification advances for the first time since 2001.
A proof of closure. Path 4 shows $6802$ is final — the harder, more definitive ending, and a result about conference graphs well beyond this single tuple.

Either ending is publishable; neither is reached yet. The tools to attempt both are catalogued next, in Chapter 11 — every image, script and data file, ready to run.
