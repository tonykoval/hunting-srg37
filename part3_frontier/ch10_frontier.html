10.2   The plan, ranked

1. Recursive SMS cube-and-conquer on the seven towers  (the live campaign — 50–70× faster since 2026-07-01)

The canonicity-propagated cube-and-conquer of $\S$4.4 has run for months and converged: the search reduced to the seven un-splittable towers — at cube size $16$, 1653 of 1792 sub-cubes exhausted, ${\sim}87$ hard, $0$ new (2026-06-29). What was "cluster-scale" then changed character on 2026-07-01 with two engine findings:

The lex-chain lesson (2026-07-01)

Instrumenting the propagator showed the pairwise canonicity check costs only $0.4\%$ of wall time on hard cubes — the wall was pure CDCL depth. Extending the static row-lex chain from its historical cutoff (rows $1$–$15$) to all $36$ consecutive row pairs hands that ordering to the solver as unit-propagatable clauses: time-to-exhaust on hard cubes fell $98.3\,\mathrm{s} \to 2.0\,\mathrm{s}$ and $104.8 \to 1.5$ (${\sim}50$–$70\times$). Sound because every chain constraint is implied by the all-pairs row-lex canonicity the model check already enforces — the enumerated model set was verified bit-identical at $n=10$ ($29$ models) and $n=14$ ($1003$). The same trick should transfer to any row-lex SMS enumeration.

On top of that the driver is now recursive (srg37_autosplit.sh): every node gets a $600$-second budget; a timeout pushes its $16$ children ($4$ more structural cube edges) onto a shared queue. Measured shed rate: $15/16$ children of a hard cube exhaust in ${\sim}13$ seconds; the hard core isolates itself $16\times$ tighter per level. As of 2026-07-02, over $98\%$ of the tower space is permanently proven empty and the campaign runs on a single desktop (hard-child branching factor ${\sim}2$–$3$ per level is the remaining uncertainty: depth-collapse ⇒ weeks; persistent fractal ⇒ months, or distribute via distribute_srg.sh). This is the only route to a completeness proof, and if $\#6803$ is reachable by enumeration it is here.

Campaign data through the size-$24$ wave (2026-07-02 evening, running in parallel with the Conway-99 order-$7$ endgame on the same desktop): split rates $18\%$ at size $20$, $27\%$ at size $24$ — the branching factor is holding, no depth-collapse yet; a dedicated completion probe on the hardest size-$24$ node (the tower-$0$ staircase core) exceeded $8$ hours without exhausting. The completeness timeline therefore leans toward the distribute-or-new-pruning branch (the feasible-charpoly tables of Path 8 being the strongest untried pruning).

Nine days on (2026-07-11): the persistent-solver campaign with clause pools has banked $110{,}817$ exhausted-node certificates (${\sim}14$K/day on three workers), driving every branch to size $40$ and accumulating the true irreducible residue — $16{,}801$ HARD leaves at size $40$ (${\sim}13\%$ of deep nodes, stable). The accounting auditor reports CLEAN continuously, the campaign survived two host shutdowns and an adversarial code review losslessly (the review found and fixed, among fourteen items, a salvage path that could have silently swallowed a found $\#6803$ in a killed log), and a self-healing supervisor now relaunches every engine hourly without human attention. The endgame decision is unchanged in kind, sharpened in number: grind the residue solo (months), warm-escalate it (the pools' instant-refutation effect makes those $16{,}801$ leaves far cheaper than their count suggests), or shard it across machines (kit ready). Zero candidates, zero false emissions, and — post-review — zero known mechanisms by which a hit could be lost.

The residue wall (2026-07-13) — seven levers, seven verdicts in one day

Twelve days in, the endgame decision got its data — and every brute-force branch failed it. The open residue stood at $14{,}827$ HARD leaves at size $40$ (dedup-corrected; growing as the main grind surfaces more towers, $121{,}708$ certificates banked). Seven escalation levers were tested against it in a single day, each to a clean verdict:

  1. Budget $\times 10$ ($600$ s, warm pools): $0/24$ leaves closed. The pools' instant-refutation effect, decisive on ordinary nodes, does not touch this class.
  2. Orbitopal fixing (satsuma 1.4, the TACAS-2026 Distinguished Paper method): dejavu finds zero residual formula symmetry — the full row-lex chain + gauge + cube units is already symmetry-saturated. A null result that doubles as a validation of the encoding.
  3. kissat 4.0.4 on the exported cube CNF: timeout at $1200$ s.
  4. CaDiCaL 3.0 (BVA-by-default, the SAT Competition 2025 UNSAT-track winner): timeout at $1200$ s. Solver vintage is not the wall.
  5. $16\times$ deeper splitting (the four missing first-ten pairs, size $44$): only ${\sim}11\%$ of children close — the $15/16$ shed rate that powers the whole recursive campaign collapses on this class. Each hard leaf becomes ${\sim}14$ hard children: splitting grows the residue.
  6. Run-to-completion probe: two known-hard leaves, warm pools, no verdict after $4$ hours each. True leaf cost exceeds $4$ h ⇒ the residue is $>2{,}800$ box-days before growth — beyond any realistic shard fleet.
  7. A fresh split axis (edges among vertices $10$–$13$, untouched by the $40$-edge basis): $0/64$ children closed. The towers are hard along every tested cube direction, exactly as the isolated-vertex gauge below predicts.

The conclusion is sharper than "months vs. distribute": completing the enumeration is no longer a compute problem. What remains is algorithmic — canonical pruning of partial assignments (the RCL/SAT-nauty route, untouched by all seven verdicts), a semantically different decomposition than edge cubes, or an honest write-up of this frontier. The main grind continues regardless: every certificate is permanent, and the towers it maps are exactly where a $\#6803$, if it exists, must live.

Day two at the wall (2026-07-14) — the proof-system axis, march cubes, and the pool-poison

The next day tested the two axes the first seven verdicts never touched, with the help of a genuinely new tool for each.

Changing the proof system. All seven refuted levers lived inside resolution — and the instance's heart is the exactly-$18$ counting layer, precisely where resolution needs exponentially long proofs and cutting planes often needs only polynomial ones. A new exporter (--export-opb-cube) emits the exact live cube instance with the cardinality native — $3.9$ MB of pseudo-Boolean constraints instead of $26$ MB of totalizer CNF — validated by decode-verifying a $n=10$ model and confirming the provably-nonexistent $n=12$ case UNSAT. The verdict from roundingsat: UNKNOWN at one hour, UNKNOWN at the full four-hour parity budget. The counting-layer hope did not materialize; the proof-system axis is dead at any budget this campaign can afford.

Letting lookahead pick the split. Every prior split used fixed edge bases chosen by hand. march_cu — the classic cube-and-conquer cuber — picked its own splitting variables by propagation lookahead from all $159$k (edges, agree-variables, chain auxiliaries) and decomposed the four-hour leaf into $258$ cubes in seconds, refuting several subtrees during cubing itself. $42\%$ of the cubes close in under $30$ seconds — the best per-budget decomposition found in the entire campaign (fixed edges managed $11\%$). But the remaining $58\%$ resist kissat and the warm engine identically: the hard core is decomposition-invariant and solver-invariant. The fractal does not care how you cut it.

The pool-poison. The day's real discovery came from an anomaly: fed the same march cubes, the warm engine scored $0/24$ where cold kissat scored $10/24$ — worse tooling should not beat better. A three-arm isolation pinned it in minutes: the same cube closes in $3$ seconds bare and sits unsolved after $130$ seconds with the $20$ MB warm clause pool loaded. The pools that make ordinary edge-cube nodes instant — the campaign's proudest optimization — bury re-attacks that enter the space along a different axis. A scope check re-ran the probe leaves pool-free: still HARD at $600$ s, so the wall itself stands uncontaminated — but every future escalation, probe, and slice run is now pool-free by default. The lesson generalizes: an optimization tuned for one traversal regime can be actively hostile in another, and only a controlled isolation tells you which regime you are in.

The plan from here. Three cards remain, in order: (1) the RCL canonicity port — prefix-hereditary canonical pruning of partial assignments, the one order-of-magnitude idea in the literature untouched by all eleven verdicts; (2) the cluster probe fleet — self-contained docker shards (kit ready) running disjoint residue leaves to completion at $96$-hour caps, pool-free, to buy the leaf-cost distribution that prices any brute-force ending; (3) the SAT-2026 harvest (July 20–23) — the cardinality-encoding and clause-sharing camera-readies land days from this writing. The main grind continues throughout: certificates are permanent, and the towers are still exactly where a $\#6803$ must live.

Day three at the wall (2026-07-16) — split the counting proof itself

March's most useful split variable, $17221$, turned out not to be an edge but an internal totalizer counter. That observation produced the first decomposition to keep its strength recursively: four balanced counters for the row-pair $(2,3)$ closed $13/16$ branches of a calibration root, and four child counters closed $43/48$ descendants. An unrelated pair managed only $2/14$, so the winning rule is specific: descend the same effective counting tree rather than stacking arbitrary constraints.

The first production sample covered $64$ HARD@40 roots. Across its $1024$ top slices, $875$ were UNSAT ($85.45\%$), $149$ were promoted, and no SAT candidate appeared. Every UNSAT verdict arrived within $9$ seconds (median $3$ seconds), making a $10$-second first tier lossless on this sample. At the latest depth-$3$ checkpoint, $1183/2384$ slices had completed: $1064$ UNSAT, $119$ TIMEOUT, $0$ SAT ($89.9\%$ closure among completed jobs).

The cluster path is now Rust-audited end to end. It reconstructs each CE40 root from its numeric index and independently proves that every recursive family contains all truth assignments of the same fresh variables; verified nodes may have $2$, $4$, $8$, or $16$ children, and job names alone are never accepted as a certificate. A second Rust pilot keeps the $16$ siblings of one root in a single CaDiCaL under assumptions. It reproduced Kissat's exact $13/16$ status vector while using $35.3$ solver-seconds instead of $92$ ($2.6\times$ on that root). Clause state is discarded between parent partitions, preserving the pool-isolation lesson from day two. The conqueror now accepts explicit ancestor definitions and resume ledgers and is the production engine for the next recursive layer. A broader four-root check matched all $64/64$ independent Kissat statuses and used $73.9$ solver-seconds instead of $236$ ($3.19\times$).

The representative survivor then revealed the decisive shape: twelve successive four-counter splits each closed $15/16$, leaving one narrow hard path rather than a branching tree. Splitting the next four counters as two certified four-way stages took about $36$ seconds, versus $50$--$59$ seconds as one sixteen-way stage. Production therefore asks two semantic questions at a time, a measured $25$--$35\%$ improvement, and completes alternating edge thresholds that a uniform-offset sweep would miss. Rotating early to pair $(2,4)$ was worse ($2/4$ hard versus $1/4$), so the engine finishes the active $(2,3)$ subcounts first.

At the observed rates the current $18$K-root residue remains provisionally in the low-thousands of CPU-hours, but the deepest tail now grows nearly linearly and its measured cost has fallen by roughly one third. Four restart-safe staircase supervisors are armed behind the current depth-$3$ queues. Cluster compute is useful again, but only because the semantic split changed the cost model. The independent coverage ledger is CLEAN at $123{,}238$ exhausted nodes, $18{,}171$ HARD@40 leaves, $11{,}370$ queued, and three in flight.

Why the staircase towers are un-splittable — the isolated-vertex gauge

A structural fact surfaced on 2026-07-02 while probing seeded searches: every regular two-graph has a carrier with an isolated vertex (switch at the neighbourhood of any point), and that carrier's row $0$ is all zeros — so it satisfies $e[0,k]=0$ for every $k$, i.e. it lies in the all-zeros lexicographic cube at every cube depth. The perpetual hard child of each tower (the "$j=0$ staircase") is therefore not an accident of the solver: cube $0$ provably contains a representative labeling of every two-graph class — known and unknown — at every split level. There is no "aim at a known graph's cube" shortcut, and the staircase can only be settled by genuine refutation depth. (Validated at $n=46$, where the seeded window search escapes seed classes — the analogous $38$-point windows are switching-rigid, so at $37/38$ the enumeration remains the only path.)

2. GPU stochastic-local-search finder  (launched 2026-07-02)

A regular two-graph(38) is a $\pm1$ Seidel matrix with $S^2 = 37\,I$, so the hunt for one is the minimisation of the defect $f(S)=\sum_{idefect-repair move — a variable-depth chain that descends $D=S^2-37I$ directly — recovers an $8$-flip-perturbed Paley$(37)$ all the way back to $f=0$ in validation. It can only ever find a graph, never prove one absent, so it is the complement to Path 1, not a substitute: a cheap parallel shot at a witness while the CPU grind chases completeness.

Now live (srg37_sls_sweep2.ps1): the finder runs on the RTX 5060 Ti, pinned not to the old $16$-edge cubes but to the deepest surviving subspace of the recursive campaign — the seven tower staircase cores at cube size $24$ ($678$ free Seidel entries), rotating hourly with fresh seeds and the defect-repair chain enabled. If $\#6803$ exists, the fractal argument says this is where it hides; every hit is CPU re-verified and deduped against the $6802$ catalog before any claim. Ops note (hard-won): under CPU contention the CPU-side Lin–Kernighan chain starves and can wedge a run for hours — the v2 sweep throttles it (depth $40$, every $1000$ iterations), doubles the population to $8192$ (shifting work onto the GPU), and wraps every run in a $75$-minute watchdog so the tower rotation can never stall silently. Zero hits so far.

3. Rust extension backtracker with bitset propagation  (most actionable)

We already hold $30{,}122$ concrete unaccounted neighbourhoods ($\S$3.3). A compiled backtracker (popcount(adj[i] & adj[j]) for common neighbours, masked scans for degree saturation) can test thousands of them per CPU-day. Any single extension is a discovery; systematic failure starts to quantify completeness. This needs no cluster and no budget — just the multi-day implementation begun in orbit-gen v1.0.514.

4. Verifier-coupled learned policy  (research-grade)

The LLM frameworks failed by asking for a one-shot answer. Couple generation to the cheap verifier instead: fine-tune a small model on (graph, valid?) pairs and use it as the policy in an MCTS over partial adjacency matrices, expanding only branches the propagator has not killed. This is the unspent llm_mcts_search.py framework; it is the most speculative and the most interesting.

5. A completeness proof  (the other way to win)

Failing to find $\#6803$ is not the same as proving it absent. A genuine classification — closing the million unaccounted neighbourhoods, e.g. via a Lasserre/SDP hierarchy bound or a structured exhaustive two-graph enumeration — would settle the question in the opposite direction. This is an open problem in finite geometry in its own right, and the hardest item on the list.

6. Star-complement enumeration  (exact, untried)

The eigenvalue $s=(-1-\sqrt{37})/2$ has multiplicity $18$, so a graph realising it is determined by a $19$-vertex star complement $H$ (a subgraph not having $s$ as an eigenvalue) together with extension vertices compatible via $b^\top(sI-A_H)^{-1}b = s$. Enumerate the spectrum-compatible $H$, then seek an $18$-clique in the extension-compatibility graph; any clique reconstructs a 37-vertex SRG. The method (Cvetković–Rowlinson–Simić) is exact, assumes no automorphism, and gives a proof certificate if the $H$'s are exhausted — and it is GPU-batchable with the spectrum and PSD kernels we already have. Genuinely untried here.

7. SDP / interlacing cube-pruning  (kills hard cubes)

Every principal submatrix of the Seidel matrix $S$ ($S^2=37I$) has all its eigenvalues in $[-\sqrt{37},\sqrt{37}]$ (Cauchy interlacing). A small semidefinite feasibility test on a partially-assigned principal submatrix — is there a completion with $-\sqrt{37}\,I \preceq P \preceq \sqrt{37}\,I$? — can refute a hard cube that SAT cannot split, and the tests batch on the GPU PSD/Cholesky kernels. Not a global proof on its own, but a cube-killer that complements the SMS search on exactly the ${\sim}87$ residual cubes.

8. Feasible-characteristic-polynomial tables  (new — from the 2026 literature)

A strict sharpening of Path 7, surfaced by a 2026-07 literature sweep. For any order-$m$ principal submatrix $S_m$ of the putative conference matrix with $m \ge 20$, interlacing against the two-eigenvalue spectrum $\{\pm\sqrt{37}\}$ forces the exact factorisation $$\chi_{S_m}(x) \;=\; (x^2-37)^{\,m-19}\cdot q(x),$$ where $q$ is a monic integer polynomial of degree $38-m$, real-rooted with all roots in $[-\sqrt{37},\sqrt{37}]$ — further filtered by $a_1=0$, $a_2=-\binom{m}{2}$, and the Greaves–Yatsyna $2$-adic congruences on $\chi(x-1)$. The 2025 LP enumeration algorithms of Greaves–Syatriadi (arXiv:2504.09241, the machinery behind the equiangular- line maxima in dimensions $17$–$20$) make the complete finite table of feasible polynomials precomputable for every level $m=20,\dots,37$: any partial matrix whose completed principal block falls off-table is soundly refuted. Strictly stronger than interval interlacing $+$ the de Caen identity at those levels; an endgame propagator for exactly the deep hard cubes the recursion isolates. Untried — the top implementation candidate if the fractal persists.

Tried and closed since the first edition (2026-07-02): the CDCL(⊕) solver Xorcle (arXiv:2605.15002) — native parity-equation branching with $\mathrm{Res}(\oplus)$ learning, on paper the exact match for this parity-structured wall — was benchmarked on exported cube instances (--export-cnf-cube). Verdict: the prototype stores every literal as a dense bit-vector over all variables (${\sim}25$ GB at $n=38$) and, at $n=14$ where memory fits, produced no answer in $300$ s where the SMS engine fully enumerates in $10$ s. The Tseitin-scaling result does not transfer to cardinality-parity mixes; the dead-ledger grows by one honest entry.

10.3   Two ways this ends

The two endings — one decision procedure

The live campaign (Path 1) settles the question both ways. A new graph. Any sub-cube — or a GPU restart (Path 2), or the extension backtracker (Path 3) — emits a valid $\mathrm{SRG}(37,18,8,9)$ whose canonical form is absent from the catalog. The count becomes (at least) $6803$ and the classification advances for the first time since 2001.
A proof of closure. Every one of the $1792$ sub-cubes — including the ${\sim}87$ hard ones, recursively split to a real UNSAT certificate — exhausts with zero new descendants. Then $6802$ is exactly final: the first open case in the classification of regular two-graphs is closed, a result about conference graphs well beyond this single tuple. (An expired timeout is UNKNOWN, not a refutation — closure needs the hard cubes genuinely driven to UNSAT.)

Either ending is publishable; neither is reached yet. The tools to attempt both are catalogued next, in Chapter 11 — every image, script and data file, ready to run.
