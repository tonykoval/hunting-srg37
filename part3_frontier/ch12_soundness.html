Chapter 12 · Part III Is the Search Correct?

A hunt that returns "no new graph" is worthless unless the hunt is sound — unless every graph it claims to have ruled out really is absent. Most search logs never audit their own tooling. This one does, and the audit turned up exactly one real trap, which we disarmed. This is the chapter that lets the eventual answer — graph or proof of closure — be trusted.

12.1   Two ways a search can lie

An isomorph-free enumeration can be wrong in two directions:

  • False UNSAT — it reports a cube empty when a graph actually lives there. Catastrophic: it would make us miss $\#6803$ and wrongly declare closure.
  • False suppression — it finds a genuine new graph but mislabels it as already-in-catalog and discards it.

Both come down to one thing: the canonicity machinery. The SMS propagator decides which partial assignments to prune, and the dedup step decides which descendants are "new". A bug in either silently corrupts the whole result. So we tested both — not by inspection, but by running controlled experiments to exhaustion.

12.2   The orbit-set-equality test

The propagator keeps only the lexicographically minimal representative of each isomorphism class. The fear: that "minimal representative" is implemented incorrectly and drops whole classes. The test: run the search two ways — the fast canonicity and a deliberately redundant one — on small conference cases where the answer is fully enumerable, and check the sets of distinct two-graph orbits are identical.

$N=14,\ \alpha=6$ (Paley 13)1003 raw models26 raw modelsboth → 26 ✓
"1003 models for one graph" is not a bug

The unique Paley(13) two-graph shows up as $1003$ raw models under the weak canonicity and only $26$ orbit classes after dedup. That factor is redundancy — relabellings of the same orbits — not missed graphs. The two searches canonicalise to the identical set of distinct orbits. The search is sound; the weak canonicity is merely slow, which is why deep cubes time out (Chapter 4) rather than silently lie.

12.3   The trap we found (and disarmed)

A column-major lex-leader canonicity, CANON_CCL, is a genuine ${\sim}38\times$ speedup, and the orbit-set test above passes for it over a full cube partition. It is tempting to deploy. We did not — and here is the precise reason, which is a useful cautionary tale about mixing two symmetry-breaking schemes.

Why CANON_CCL is unsound with the towers

The seven-tower reduction (Chapter 4) proves the non-tower cubes empty using row-lex symmetry breaking. CANON_CCL switches that off and makes column-major minimality the sole canonicity. The column-minimal representative of an orbit can then fall into a cube the row-lex argument already pruned — so the orbit is represented nowhere that gets searched. Over the full partition this is harmless (every orbit's representative is somewhere); restricted to the seven towers it is a false UNSAT. Two individually-sound prunings, composed, become unsound. The live run therefore stays on the slower row-lex canonicity, and the $38\times$ speedup is left on the table on purpose.

12.4   Dedup cannot suppress a real graph

The "is this descendant new?" test canonicalises every candidate with nauty's labelg and looks it up in the 6802-string catalog. Because the nauty canonical form is a complete invariant — two graphs share it iff they are isomorphic — a genuinely new graph cannot collide with a catalog entry. False suppression is therefore impossible by construction, provided the catalog file itself is correct (it was rebuilt and re-canonicalised from the three published sources; Chapter 3).

12.5   What the sound search has proven so far

Because the search is sound, every "empty" sub-cube is a real theorem: no regular two-graph on 38 lies in this corner of the space. The live state (a snapshot — re-derived from the result log, not a guess):

Seven-tower progress (snapshot 2026-06-29)
exhausted 1653 · hard 87 · pending 52 · new 0  of 1792

Each tile is a block of sub-cubes in one of the seven towers. Green = proven empty, amber = hard (24 h timeout, needs deeper splitting), grey = pending. More than 92% of the space is now provably empty; the search converges on a fractal core of ${\sim}87$ hard cubes.

The decision procedure

Drive every one of the 1792 sub-cubes — including the hard residual, recursively split to a real UNSAT certificate — to exhaustion with zero new descendants, and the catalog is proven complete: exactly $6802$, no $\#6803$. Or emit one non-catalog descendant, and that graph is $\#6803$. The soundness results of this chapter are what make both conclusions trustworthy. (An expired timeout is UNKNOWN, not a refutation — closure needs the hard cubes genuinely closed.)
