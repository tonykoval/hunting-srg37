Chapter 13 · Part III The Engine — one week, three orders of magnitude

In the first week of July 2026 the live campaign's engine was rebuilt four times, each rebuild validated before deployment and each dead end closed by a controlled experiment. This chapter is the engineering diary of that week — what made the search ${\sim}1000\times$ faster, what didn't work and how we know, and how the whole edifice was then adversarially audited. It is the chapter to read if you want to run a search like this yourself.

13.1   Where the time actually goes

The week began with an instrumented measurement that overturned the working assumption. The SMS canonicity propagator — the sophisticated part — was charged with the slowness of the hard cubes. Timing counters said otherwise: on hard cubes the pairwise canonicity check cost 0.4% of wall time. The wall was raw CDCL depth. Every subsequent win came from the same move applied to a different layer: take knowledge the solver eventually derives, and hand it over in a form it can use immediately.

LeverMechanismMeasured effect
Full static lex chain (LEX_CHAIN_ROWS=37) row-lex ordering as unit-propagatable clauses instead of propagator callbacks $98.3\,\mathrm{s}\to 2.0\,\mathrm{s}$, $104.8\to 1.5$ on hard cubes (${\sim}50$–$70\times$); model sets bit-identical at $n=10,14$
Persistent solver (cube literals as assumptions) one CaDiCaL instance per subtree; learned clauses survive across the DFS easy nodes $12\,\mathrm{s}\to 0.03\,\mathrm{s}$; subtree-to-depth-24 $802\,\mathrm{s}$ vs ${\sim}14{,}200\,\mathrm{s}$ (${\sim}17\times$); warm clauses halve the hard-node rate
Clause pools (write_dimacs → BASE_CNF) derived units + irredundant DB + blocking clauses persist across subtrees and machines size-40 leaves that were 60 s timeouts fall at $0.0\,\mathrm{s}$ once pools mature; pool file = portable warm-start

Two soundness arguments carry all three. The chain is implied by the all-pairs row-lex canonicity the model check enforces, so adding it cannot lose a class. The pool is an equisatisfiable strengthening: assumptions are decisions in CaDiCaL, so no assumption-conditioned fact can ever become a root-level unit — verified in the vendored solver source during the audit (§13.4). Blocking clauses persisting across runs is not a bug but the campaign's dedup memory: every blocked labeling was verified, descended, and tested against the catalog at first contact.

The tell-tale signals (worth memorizing)

Each null announced itself with a distinctive symptom before any deep analysis: a supposedly-hard instance returning UNSAT in 0.0 seconds means your constraints are not the ones you think (wrong edge basis, foreign canonical order); a positive control returning 0 models means a pruning layer is unsound for the mode you are running; violation counters exploding while the trail stays shallow means two symmetry-breaking notions are fighting. Cheap controls convert all three from silent theorem-killers into same-day bug reports.

13.3   The isolated-vertex gauge, and why the staircase is the whole game

The week also produced the structural lemma of Chapter 10 (§10.2): switching any two-graph at the neighbourhood of a point yields a carrier with an isolated vertex, whose row is all zeros — so every class has a representative in the all-zeros lexicographic cube at every depth. The perpetually-hard staircase is therefore not solver weakness but theorem: it provably hosts a copy of everything, including $\#6803$ if it exists. Two consequences run the campaign: the staircase residue is exactly where the find-side probability concentrates (row-lex-minimal representatives have sparse early rows), and no "aim at a known graph's cube" shortcut can exist.

13.4   The audit — correctness as a checked property

With months of certificates accumulating, the final act of the week was an adversarial audit: three independent reviews of the mathematical core (propagator clause construction, acceptance, chain, cuts), the persistent- solver machinery (assumption lifecycle, pool semantics, blocking order), and the certificate accounting (the full partition tree). The mathematical core came back clean, with mechanical evidence: a 30,000-trail stateful fuzzer (81,246 emitted clauses, zero kill a canonical assignment), exhaustive acceptance verification at small $n$, and a written proof that the string-minimal labeling survives every pairwise constraint and the gauge.

The audit's real catch was operational: four silent-failure mechanisms that could lose coverage or — worse — a discovery, while still printing EXHAUSTED. A missing catalog file degraded to "smoke mode" and would have blocked a found $\#6803$ without ever descending it; a failed nauty subprocess did the same for one model; a killed worker destroyed its popped work item; and ten nodes had in fact been orphaned by earlier driver restarts. All four are now hard failures or auto-recovered, the ten orphans were requeued, the deleted round-2 tower certificates were re-proven (249/249), and a retroactive sweep confirmed all 5,242 historical processes ran with the full catalog. The accounting itself is guarded by srg37_cover_check.py, which walks the entire split tree and currently reports:

The auditor's line

RESULT: CLEAN — every tower node is certified, split-covered, hard-ledgered, queued, or in flight — exit 0

The driver now runs this check before it may print a completion banner. When the queue finally drains, the claim "exactly 6802" will not rest on a log line a human believes, but on a program that has already caught real gaps refusing to pass false ones.

13.5   What a reader should take away

Three transferable habits. Measure before optimizing — the propagator "bottleneck" was 0.4% of wall, and the real fix (the chain) took an afternoon. Give every idea a control that can kill it same-day — the week ran seven experiments, deployed three, and lost no compute to the four that failed, because each failure was caught by a designed signal. Audit the boring parts — nothing in the elegant mathematics was broken; everything dangerous lived in file loading, process kills, and bookkeeping, exactly where nobody looks and exactly where a theorem dies.

13.6   Postscript: the fourth habit — novelty-check before claiming

The week after this chapter was first written, the discipline earned a fourth entry. A literature check run before writing up a "theorem" caught, in one afternoon, that the result in question — an automorphism-order exclusion at another parameter set — had been published fifteen years earlier; the same check re-attributed a second in-house result, and a third had already been caught the same way (a "new" graph that was Spence's entry #39, exposed by full canonical comparison after a plausible-looking invariant argued it was new). Three near-misclaims, total cost of prevention: hours. The corollary for computational hunts is sharp: search programs rediscover the literature by default, because they attack exactly the cases earlier authors found attackable; the only reliable boundary between "verification" and "theorem" is a citation-level check done before — never after — the announcement. The productive twist: the same check located the case the literature explicitly left unfinished, and the campaign is now aimed there instead — at a result no one has, rather than one everyone had.

13.7   Postscript II: the day of seven verdicts (2026-07-13)

Two days later the experiment discipline of §13.2 paid out its full value in a single day: seven escalation levers against the terminal residue — $10\times$ budget, orbitopal fixing, two state-of-the-art external solvers, $16\times$ deeper splitting, a run-to-completion probe, and a fresh split axis — each designed as a small measured pilot, each refuted before lunch could be wasted on it at scale (the full account is in Chapter 10). Note the shape: six of the seven cost under an hour of setup because the engine already exposed the right seams (exported cube CNFs, env-switchable bases, warm pools). Cheap decisive negatives are a feature of the architecture, not luck. Two operational scars from the same day, both recurrences of known traps, both worth engraving: a process-liveness probe that contains its own search pattern will happily report a dead engine alive (the self-match illusion — probe wrapper-free, always); and a shell script must never be edited while a live interpreter is executing it — bash reads incrementally, and the running pilot parsed the shifted bytes and crashed its own completion banner.

13.8   Postscript III: the anomaly that was better than a result (2026-07-14)

Day two at the residue wall produced clean negatives on the two remaining axes (a cutting-planes solver on native-cardinality exports; lookahead-chosen cubes — Chapter 10 has the numbers), but its most valuable output came from a number that made no sense: fed the same $24$ cubes, the warm engine — pools, propagator, chain, every advantage — scored $0/24$ where bare kissat scored $10/24$. Worse tooling beating better tooling is not a result, it is a fire alarm. The response pattern is worth engraving. First, refuse to average it away — an anomaly is information, never noise to be diluted with more runs. Second, isolate by construction: one cube, three arms — propagator without pool ($3$ s, closed), pool without propagator ($130$ s, unsolved), neither ($2$ s, closed) — and the guilty component names itself in four minutes. Third, scope the damage before rewriting history: if pools poison these re-attacks, did they poison yesterday's wall verdicts too? A pool-free re-run of the probe leaves said no — still hard bare — so the wall stands and the discovery lands as an ops rule (pools for the main grind's traversal regime, never for re-entry along a different axis) rather than a retraction. An optimization tuned to one search regime had quietly become an anchor in another; without the anomaly-first discipline it would have been recorded as "fusion refuted" and the real mechanism never found.

13.9   Postscript IV: split the counter, not another edge (2026-07-16)

The next useful idea was hiding inside the encoding. March selected variable $17221$ again and again while choosing lookahead cubes. That variable was not an edge at all: it was a node in one of the totalizers that counts agreements between a pair of rows. This suggested a different decomposition. Instead of fixing yet more arbitrary edges of a hard size-$40$ cube, split on statements such as ``at least $t$ vertices in this contiguous block agree with rows $2$ and $3$.'' These are semantic, balanced questions about the obstruction the solver is actually trying to satisfy.

PilotClosedInterpretation
Four agreement counters for pair $(2,3)$ $13/16$ in $120$ s Most branches died in $0$--$3$ s.
Recursive counters inside the three survivors $43/48$ in $120$ s The same semantic tree kept its resolving power.
An unrelated row pair $(4,5)$ $2/14$ Not every semantic split is useful; retire weak axes quickly.

This changed the distributed plan. A standalone Rust tool now constructs and audits the theorem-critical partition. A cluster shard contains a generic chain-strengthened CNF, an exact $40$-edge root assignment, and all sixteen agreement-counter children. A root may be banked as exhausted only when all sixteen tags are present and UNSAT; timeout leaves remain explicit residue for the next semantic level. The jobs deliberately run without clause pools: the pool anomaly of §13.8 showed that learned state from the edge-cube traversal can slow a cross-axis re-entry by two orders of magnitude. The first production kit holds $64$ hard roots ($1024$ independent jobs), split into four restart-safe Docker archives.

A second idea was tested in parallel: recursive canonical labeling (RCL), adapted from the 2026 SAT-nauty work. With vertex $0$ isolated as the switching gauge, the implementation kept one representative at orders $10$ and $14$; at order $14$ it collapsed the old $1003$ labeled models to one graph in $3.6$ seconds. But order $18$ still timed out after $600$ seconds and made about $2.8$ million nauty calls. The mathematics is sound and the quotient is excellent; this raw prototype is not yet the order-$38$ engine. The practical order is therefore: deploy semantic-totalizer shards now, recurse only on their timeout leaves, and improve RCL's prefix scheduling and incremental canonical-state reuse as a separate research line.

Current frontier

No new SRG(37) and no completeness theorem has appeared in the literature. The live audited search contains $122{,}940$ exhausted nodes, $18{,}123$ explicit hard leaves, $11{,}371$ queued nodes, and no coverage gaps. The semantic split is the first post-wall method to preserve high closure at a second recursive level, so cluster time should be spent there rather than on another uniform budget increase.
